# Gemini 2026-06-01

Setting up an e-commerce operation in Switzerland requires navigating a unique mix of general contract law, mandatory consumer protections, and strict unfair competition rules.

Under Swiss law, contract regulations are federalized. This means the **Swiss Code of Obligations (CO)** applies nationwide, and individual cantons—like the **Canton of Vaud**—do not have their own separate contract codes. However, Vaud does have localized administrative and enforcement nuances you should keep in mind.

---

## 1. Core Articles of the Swiss Code of Obligations (CO)

When drafting your General Terms and Conditions (T&Cs or *Conditions Générales de Vente - CGV*) and your online checkout flow, the following CO articles are non-negotiable:

### Formation of the Contract

* **Art. 1 CO (Mutual Expression of Intent):** A contract is formed only when both parties agree on the essential terms. Your checkout process must clearly show the moment the customer makes a binding offer (usually by clicking an order button) and the moment you accept it (e.g., an automated confirmation email).
* **Art. 7 Para. 3 CO (Display of Merchandise):** Crucially for e-commerce, displaying an item online with a price tag is legally considered a binding *offer* by the seller under Swiss law, unless you explicitly state otherwise.
> **Implementation Tip:** You must include a clause in your T&Cs stating that the product display on your website is non-binding (*sans engagement* / *invitatio ad offerendum*), and that the contract is only finalized once you send a formal order confirmation.



### Product Warranties & Liability

* **Art. 199 & 210 CO (Statutory Warranty):** By default, Swiss law provides a **2-year statutory warranty** for defective goods. You are allowed to modify this in your T&Cs (e.g., restricting it to a replacement or repair instead of a full refund), but **Art. 199 CO** states that any clause excluding or limiting liability for defects is entirely void if you intentionally deceived the buyer about the defect.
* **Art. 100 CO (Limitation of Liability):** You cannot exclude liability for "gross negligence" or "intent" in your T&Cs. You can only limit your liability for ordinary/light negligence.

---

## 2. The Heavy Hitters: LCD and Price Disclosure

While the CO dictates the bones of the contract, the **Federal Act against Unfair Competition (LCD)** governs how your webshop must interact with consumers. Failing to adhere to these rules makes your T&Cs legally void:

* **Art. 3 Para. 1 lit. s LCD (The E-Commerce Transparency Rule):** You must clearly display your identity (company name, physical address, and email) on the site. Your checkout flow **must** allow the customer to review their order and correct errors before placing it, and you must confirm the order via email immediately.
* **Art. 8 LCD (Unfair T&C Clauses):** This article bans the use of T&Cs that cause a "significant and unjustified imbalance" between the rights and obligations of the merchant and the consumer, to the detriment of the consumer. If a clause is deemed completely unfair or hidden, a court will simply strike it out.

---

## 3. What about the Right of Withdrawal (Returns)?

Unlike the European Union (which mandates a 14-day return policy), **Swiss statutory law does not grant a legal right of return or a "cooling-off" period for standard online purchases.** Once a customer clicks buy, the contract is binding.

* You are legally allowed to state "All sales are final" in your T&Cs.
* However, offering a voluntary 7 to 14-day return window is highly expected by Swiss consumers as a matter of commercial practice.

---

## 4. Does the Canton of Vaud Prescribe Anything Special?

**No, Vaud does not have a separate Code of Obligations or specific local contract laws.** Contract law is strictly a federal matter in Switzerland. However, operating your business out of Vaud brings a few administrative and enforcement aspects to light:

### Price Indication Ordinance (OIP) Enforcement

While the rules on how prices must be displayed (including VAT, mandatory fees, etc.) are federal, the enforcement of these transparency rules is handled by cantonal authorities. In Vaud, the **Service de la promotion économique et de l'innovation (SPEI)** or local consumer protection watchdogs monitor businesses to ensure web prices don't mislead the public.

### Language Expectations

While not a strict statutory law for private B2C contracts, Vaud is entirely French-speaking. If you are targeting consumers in Vaud, your entire checkout flow, transaction emails, and T&Cs **should be available in French**. If a legal dispute arises and your T&Cs are only in English or German—and you actively marketed to a French-speaking population—a local cantonal court may rule that the customer could not have reasonably understood or consented to the restrictive clauses in your T&Cs.

### Administrative Checklist for Vaud

If your business is physically headquartered in Vaud, ensure you have ticked these boxes:

* **Commercial Registry (*Registre du Commerce*):** If your e-commerce entity is an Sàrl or SA, it must be registered with the *Registre du commerce du Canton de Vaud*. If you are a sole proprietorship, registration becomes mandatory once your revenue hits CHF 100,000.
* **VAT (*TVA*):** If your total worldwide turnover exceeds CHF 100,000, you must register for Swiss VAT. Remember that on your Swiss invoices, you must explicitly use the term **"TVA"** (or MWST/IVA)—using the English acronym "VAT" alone is technically not compliant with Swiss tax formatting guidelines.

To make your General Terms and Conditions (T&Cs / *CGV*) legally robust and compliant with Swiss federal law, you cannot rely on generic, copy-pasted templates from other countries (especially the EU). Swiss law gives you a lot of contractual freedom, but it also penalizes lack of transparency.

Your Swiss e-commerce T&Cs should be structured around these essential clauses to protect your business and satisfy the Federal Act against Unfair Competition (LCD) and the Code of Obligations (CO):

---

## 1. Scope of Application (*Champ d'application*)

This clause defines exactly who the contract is between and establishes that these T&Cs apply to every transaction made on your website.

* **What it must state:** It must clearly list your full corporate identity (Company name, legal form e.g., Sàrl/SA, physical address, email, and IDE/UID registration number).
* **The Swiss Nuance:** It should state that any conflicting terms provided by the buyer are expressly rejected unless agreed upon in writing.

## 2. Formation of the Contract (*Conclusion du contrat*)

This is the most critical protective clause for an online merchant in Switzerland. Under **Art. 7 para. 3 CO**, displaying an item with a price online can be viewed as a binding offer. If you make a pricing mistake (e.g., listing a CHF 2,000 laptop for CHF 20), you could be legally forced to honor it.

* **What it must state:** Explicitly state that the product display on your website is **non-binding** (*sans engagement* / *invitatio ad offerendum*).
* **The Workflow:** Define that the customer's order represents a binding offer to buy, but the contract is only officially concluded when you send a separate **Order Confirmation Email** (*Confirmation de commande*), not just the automatic payment receipt.

## 3. Prices, Shipping, and Payment Terms

Swiss price disclosure laws (OIP) are strictly enforced by cantonal authorities.

* **What it must state:** Prices must be clearly displayed in Swiss Francs (CHF) and state whether **Swiss VAT (TVA)** is included or excluded.
* **Hidden Fees:** Shipping costs, delivery surcharges, or payment processing fees (e.g., credit card or Twint fees) cannot be a surprise; they must be clearly detailed in this clause and visible before the final click.

## 4. Delivery and Retention of Title (*Réserve de propriété*)

You must manage expectations regarding shipping timelines and protect your inventory before it is paid for.

* **What it must state:** Delivery dates should be stated as *estimates* rather than strict deadlines to protect you against supply chain delays.
* **Retention of Title:** Include a clause stating that the delivered goods remain the legal property of your business until full payment has been received.

## 5. Right of Return / Withdrawal (*Droit de retour / Rétractation*)

Because Switzerland does **not** have a statutory 14-day cooling-off period for online shopping like the EU, you have complete legal freedom here. However, clarity is mandatory under the transparency guidelines of Art. 3 LCD.

* **What it must state:** You must explicitly state your policy. If sales are final, say so clearly: *"No right of return or withdrawal applies once the order confirmation is issued."*
* **If you offer returns voluntarily:** Clearly define the timeframe (e.g., 14 days), the required condition of the items (unopened, original packaging), and **who pays for the return shipping** (usually the customer).

## 6. Warranty and Liability (*Garantie et Responsabilité*)

By default, Swiss law imposes a 2-year warranty for product defects (**Art. 210 CO**). You are allowed to modify this, but you must be specific.

* **What it must state:** You can limit the statutory warranty by stating that in the event of a defect, you reserve the right to **repair the item or offer a replacement**, rather than issue an immediate refund or price reduction.
* **Limitation of Liability:** You must include a clause limiting your liability for indirect or consequential damages (like lost profits). Crucially, per **Art. 100 CO**, you must specify that liability is excluded to the maximum extent permitted by law, except for cases of intentional misconduct or gross negligence.

## 7. Data Protection (*Protection des données*)

With the revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP), you cannot hide your privacy practices.

* **What it must state:** While you should have a separate Privacy Policy, your T&Cs should include a brief clause stating that customer data is processed in accordance with Swiss data privacy regulations, and link directly to your full Privacy Policy document.

## 8. Governing Law and Jurisdiction (*Droit applicable et For juridique*)

If a legal dispute arises, you want it handled close to home under laws you understand.

* **What it must state:** State that the contract is governed exclusively by **Swiss law** (explicitly excluding the UN Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods / Vienna Sales Convention).
* **The Place of Jurisdiction:** Define the legal venue (the courts of your business seat—for example, *"The exclusive place of jurisdiction for all disputes is Lausanne, Switzerland"*).
* > **Important Note for B2C:** For true consumer contracts (B2C), the Swiss Civil Procedure Code guarantees consumers the right to sue or be sued at *their own place of residence*. Your clause specifying your local court will primarily bind business clients (B2B), but it remains standard practice to include it.



---

### A Final Warning on Incorporation

A brilliant T&Cs document is legally useless if it isn't properly incorporated into the sales process. To ensure a court enforces these clauses, your checkout page **must** force the user to actively check an un-ticked box that reads: *"I have read and agree to the General Terms and Conditions"* with a direct hyperlink to the document, before they can click the final order button.
